Situational Character

I recently spent 3 hours with a friend trying to answer the following question: “What are three words that describe your personality?”

Turns out that we didn’t really know each other that well, and that three words are far from enough words to describe a person. That being said, I think that in any given situation there are a few character traits that are particularly salient.

Take, for example, getting a meal with an old high school friend. After not catching up for years, what do you talk about? Do you just hear life updates? Do joke about old memories? Do you try to discover who’ve they become? While these (and more) are all reasonable conversation paths, the mentality you bring to the conversation heavily dictates which route you walk down. If I was curious and concrete, I’d ask a lot about the specific events that have happened. If I was nostalgic and lonely, I’d reminisce about the old times. And if I was feeling particularly visionary, I’d definitely try to piece together the human they’ve grown into.

Now contrast that with the example of debugging some React code you wrote a few days ago. If I was impatient and careless, I’d be setting myself up for a frustrating few hours. If I was detail-oriented and focused, I’d be in for a slow but satisfying burn. And maybe being rationally resourceful and patient would lead to a creative debugging session.

Finally, a fun example: a date with someone you’ve been talking to on Hinge. The possibility space here is huge: you can be ANYONE you want. Pick a few adjectives from this list: kind, curious, self-centered, thoughtful, loud, charismatic, shy, sad, and greedy. Every set that you choose creates an entirely different experience - some good, some bad, and some in between.

Importantly, the choice of adjectives is neither objectively good nor objectively bad - it’s simply good or bad for that situation, which creates good or bad experiences.

I’ve taken this thought exercise and tried to expand it into a mindfulness practice. Each time I have a major context switch, I ask myself “what type of person do I want to be in this specific situation?” This has been excellent for 1) enabling the context switching to be more thorough, and 2) noticing when I feel I’m not acting like the person I want to be.

With regards to 2), doing a quick re-evaluation leads to either an attitude or adjective adjustment. In other words, I notice suboptimal behavior (for that situation) sooner and either 1) adjust said behavior or 2) adjust what type of person I want to be in that situation.

After actively practicing for a few weeks now, I've found it to be quite useful in my life. There are a few times when I’ve struggled with whatever activity is on hand, then realized I was stuck in a different “character”. It’s been rather helpful shifting from programming → email writing, working → socializing, working → reading, and even getting in the mood for bedtime. Quite a nice sense of intentionality.

Disclaimer: This framework is predicated on the axiom that humans can shift prioritizing different dimensions without repercussions. This is not an obvious belief, as it’s quite easy to lose a “sense of self” when switching “situational character”. I've written another whole essay on this topic, Code Switching.
